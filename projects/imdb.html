Context

With Jasmine Jordan-Lake, I explored an IMDb dataset from data.world — 5,273 films and 27 features — to ask how audience preferences and industry norms have shifted across a century of cinema, and how those shifts show up in ratings and box office.

Approach

Working in R, we cleaned the raw data down to the variables that carried signal — title, year, rating, runtime, IMDb score, type, and box office — dropping incomplete rows but deliberately keeping outliers, since extreme films often tell the clearest story. That left 2,550 films, which we visualized against five objectives spanning ratings, runtime, box office, and MPAA classification.

What we found

Ratings trace a "Cinematic Golden Age" — films from the 1920s–70s score consistently high, give way to volatility through the 80s–2000s, then converge toward lower, more homogeneous ratings today. Most films cluster between about 5.8 and 7.3 (median ≈ 6.6). Box office has only a small positive tie to rating — blockbusters like Star Wars: Episode I (6.5) aren't the best-reviewed — while runtime correlates more clearly, with longer films tending to rate higher. We packaged it all into a structured deck built for a non-technical audience.
